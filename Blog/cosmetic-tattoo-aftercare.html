Aftercare  ·  Complete Guide

Cosmetic Tattoo Aftercare — The First Two Weeks

By Jessica · Allure Microblading, Auckland

All Articles

The outcome of your cosmetic tattoo is only partly determined in the studio. What happens in the two weeks that follow matters just as much. This guide covers exactly what to do and what to avoid, for brows, eyeliner, and lips.

Why Aftercare Matters So Much

Cosmetic tattooing deposits pigment into the upper layers of the skin. During the healing period, the body is actively working to close and repair that skin, which means the pigment is still settling and vulnerable to being displaced, faded, or unevenly retained. The choices you make during this window directly influence how the colour heals and how long it lasts.

Most cases of patchy, uneven, or faded results that come in for corrective work have one thing in common: aftercare that went wrong somewhere. Usually picking. Sometimes sun. Occasionally a sauna on day four.

The procedure takes a few hours. The result is built over two weeks. Both halves matter equally.

The Healing Stages — What Is Normal

Every cosmetic tattoo goes through the same general stages regardless of the treatment area. Knowing what to expect prevents the panic that often leads to bad decisions mid-heal.

Stage Days What You See
Immediate 0–2 Colour is darker and more saturated than intended. Some swelling and tenderness is normal.
Surface healing 3–7 Skin begins to flake or peel. Colour may look uneven or patchy. Do not pick.
Ghost phase 7–14 Colour appears to fade dramatically or almost disappear. This is normal. Pigment is still present beneath the surface.
Colour return 3–4 weeks Colour gradually resurfaces as the skin fully settles. True tone begins to emerge.
Final result 6–8 weeks Full healed result visible. Touch-up appointment can be assessed now.

The ghost phase is the stage that alarms clients most. Brows that looked bold on day two can look almost invisible by day ten. This is a normal part of healing and not a sign that something went wrong. The pigment that appears to have gone is still there, sitting beneath a fresh layer of skin that has not yet become fully transparent. Patience through this stage is essential.

The Universal Rules

These apply to every cosmetic tattoo, regardless of treatment area.

  • Do not pick, peel, rub, or scratch the treated area. Ever. This is the most important rule.
  • Keep the area clean and dry for the first 24 hours.
  • No swimming, saunas, steam rooms, or hot tubs for at least two weeks.
  • Avoid direct sun exposure on the treated area during healing. After healing, use SPF consistently to protect the result long-term.
  • No active skincare on or near the treated area during healing. This includes retinol, AHA, BHA, and vitamin C serums.
  • Do not apply makeup directly on the tattooed area until the skin has fully closed, typically seven to ten days.
  • Avoid intense cardio and sweating heavily for the first week. Sweat can carry bacteria into the skin and displace pigment.
  • Sleep on a clean pillowcase. Bacteria on fabric can cause irritation or infection.

Aftercare by Treatment Area

The universal rules apply everywhere, but each area has specific considerations.

Eyebrow Tattoo

  • Avoid getting brows wet in the shower for 7 days — angle your face away from the water
  • No brow makeup for 10 days
  • No hair removal (waxing, threading, tinting) for 4 weeks
  • Apply balm only if skin feels very tight or dry — a thin layer, once or twice a day
  • Avoid sleeping face-down

Eyeliner Tattoo

  • Expect swelling on days 1–2, particularly on lower liner — this is normal
  • No eye makeup for 10 days
  • Avoid contact lenses for 72 hours minimum
  • No lash extensions, lash lifts, or tinting for 4 weeks
  • Be gentle when cleansing around the eye area

Lip Blush Tattoo

  • Apply balm regularly to prevent tightness and cracking
  • No lipstick or lip products for 7 days
  • Eat carefully — avoid hot, spicy, or very oily foods in the first week
  • No kissing or friction on the lips during healing
  • If you have a history of cold sores, antiviral medication should be taken before and after the procedure

Freckles & Beauty Marks

  • Avoid touching or rubbing the area
  • Keep dry for 24 hours
  • No active skincare on the treated spots for 10 days
  • SPF is particularly important long-term, as sun exposure accelerates fading

The Balm — How and When to Use It

You will leave your appointment with an aftercare balm. The instinct is often to apply it generously and frequently. This is not the right approach. Too much product can suffocate the skin and interfere with healing, particularly for brows and eyeliner where the skin needs to breathe and flake naturally.

A thin smear, applied only when the skin feels genuinely tight or uncomfortable, is the correct approach. For lip blush, the lips are the exception. Because they do not form a dry surface crust in the same way, they benefit from more regular application to stay comfortable and prevent cracking.

If you run out of the provided balm, a plain fragrance-free petroleum jelly can be used in the same way. Avoid anything with fragrance, active ingredients, or oils during the healing window.

Less is more. A thin layer when needed, not a thick layer at all times.

What to Avoid in Your Skincare Routine

A number of commonly used skincare ingredients can interfere with healing or accelerate pigment fading. During the two-week healing period, keep all of the following away from the treated area.

  • Retinol and retinoids — accelerate cell turnover and will cause premature fading
  • AHA and BHA exfoliants such as glycolic acid and salicylic acid
  • Vitamin C serums — can lighten and destabilise pigment
  • Benzoyl peroxide acne treatments
  • Exfoliating scrubs of any kind near the treated area

Once healed, you can return to your normal skincare routine, but apply retinol and exfoliants away from the brow or lip area to protect longevity. Clients who use prescription-strength retinoids consistently will notice their cosmetic tattoo fades faster than average and may need more frequent touch-ups.

Sun Exposure and Long-Term Fading

UV exposure is the number one reason cosmetic tattoos fade faster than they should. Sun degrades pigment over time, and the effect is cumulative. During healing, direct sun on the treated area should be avoided entirely. After the skin has closed, SPF should become part of your daily routine in that area.

For brows, a mineral SPF applied over the brow area each morning significantly extends the life of the result. For lips, an SPF lip balm worn daily makes a noticeable difference to how long the colour holds. This is especially relevant in Auckland, where UV levels are high year-round.

When Something Does Not Look Right

Redness, mild tenderness, and some swelling in the first 48 hours are all normal. What is not normal is increasing redness, warmth, or discharge after day two, or any sign of infection such as pus or fever. If you notice these, contact us promptly and seek medical advice if needed. Infection is rare when aftercare instructions are followed, but it can happen.

Patchy healing, uneven colour, and areas that appear lighter than others are common and are addressed at the touch-up appointment, which is scheduled for six to eight weeks after the initial session. Do not try to fix uneven healing yourself. The touch-up exists precisely for this.

The Touch-Up Appointment

The touch-up is not optional and not an indication that something went wrong. It is the second half of the process. At six to eight weeks, Jessica assesses the healed result, adds pigment to any areas that healed lighter, refines the shape, and adjusts colour depth if needed. This is where the final result is completed.

Coming to the touch-up with accurate notes about how your skin healed is genuinely helpful. Photos taken during the ghost phase, notes on which areas faded more, and any questions about the result all make the follow-up session more efficient and the outcome more refined.
